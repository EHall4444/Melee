WHAT IS — Current Reality
	

The product as it exists today
The accidental beta is a single-page web app (one index.html file, Supabase backend) built in May 2026 for a small friend group competing on the NCAA DI Men’s Lacrosse Championship bracket. Built with AI assistance in a matter of days. It had no name, no brand, and no ambitions beyond the tournament. Its codebase is the starting point for Melee. Its friend group is the first Melee group. Its lacrosse tournament is the first Melee event. The name is retired.


What existed in the beta
	What is missing
	* Shared leaderboard
* Chatter (comments + thumbs up/down)
* View any friend’s picks
* Pick deadline with countdown
* Hand-rolled auth
* Hardcoded lacrosse bracket structure
	* Groups (persistent friend circles)
* Any event type beyond brackets
* Staggered pick reveal mechanic
* Pick change visibility
* AI-assisted event creation
* Generic schema (event/stage/contender/pick)
* Cross-event Group leaderboard
* Host customization options
	

The user we are building for
THE HOST
The person who makes things happen. They organize, they taunt, they keep score. They need Melee to be effortless to set up and impossible to ignore.
Needs: fast AI-assisted event creation (under 5 min), easy group invites, tools to poke friends who haven’t submitted. Phase 2: customization options to set the Melee’s tone.
	THE CASUAL FRIEND
Doesn’t care about lacrosse. Does care about not being left out. Will chatter if given half a chance. The dropout risk and the biggest engagement signal — and the product’s most powerful viral vector.
Needs: zero-knowledge entry (autofill), social pull-back, something to say even when losing. When the casual friend wins using autofill — that’s the moment that sells Melee to everyone watching.

WHAT IF — Design Provocations
	

What If questions open up design space without committing to solutions. Each provocation is grounded in a known tension. They are not rhetorical — each has a testable hypothesis.


Core platform provocations
Provocation
	Hypothesis
	What if the event doesn’t matter at all — only the group does?
	If we remove all event-specific language and let the social layer drive, engagement will be equal across lacrosse, the Oscars, and Survivor pools.
	What if describing an event in plain English is enough to create it?
	AI-assisted event creation will reduce Host setup time from 30+ minutes to under 5, and increase the variety of events created.
	What if picks are a provocation, not just a prediction?
	Showing who changed their picks and when — combined with staggered reveal — will generate more Chatter than the leaderboard itself.
	What if Melee is the answer to the modern social paradox?
	We have more ways to connect than ever and have never felt more alone watching things. Melee meets people on their couch, on their phone, half-watching — and gives them someone to experience it with. Not a stadium of strangers. Their actual friends. The primary tagline is the thesis: “You’re watching anyway. Now watch together.”
	What if the disengaged friend is actually the product’s best advocate?
	The casual friend who joins reluctantly, uses autofill, and ends up winning generates more word-of-mouth than the expert who always wins. Design for this moment explicitly.
	What if betting markets have already mapped every event worth predicting?
	Integrating Polymarket as an event discovery feed in Phase 2 will surface events the Host would never have thought to create.
	What if Group history is the product’s long-term moat?
	A cross-event all-time leaderboard — a Group’s permanent record across every Melee — creates identity and prevents churn more effectively than any single feature.
	What if the Host sets the emotional tone of the whole Melee?
	Giving the Host customization options (elimination style, reveal mechanic, leaderboard taunts) turns setup from a chore into a creative act. The Host becomes the DJ, not the roadie.
	What if pre-event pick transparency is more valuable than a reveal?
	Picks visible immediately on submission means chatter starts before the event. The last-minute pick change — visible to everyone — is a mind game. The deadline is the lock, not a reveal.
	What if tie-breaks are a feature, not an admin burden?
	Prop questions as default tie-breaker add personality and differentiate identical picks automatically. No Host adjudication needed. Cubs fans will still disagree on who scores first.
	What if no take-backs is a brand statement?
	Removing comment editing and adding a confirmation step before posting reinforces the whole Melee ethos: your words stand, your picks stand, own it.
	

Anti-Betting Positioning
Provocation
	Hypothesis
	What if Melee is the anti-bet?
	Positioning Melee explicitly against betting — communal vs transactional, bragging rights vs material gain, game night vs the house — creates a clearer brand identity and opens a larger market of users who would never use a betting product.
	What if Polymarket is just a data library, not a partner?
	Referencing Polymarket purely as an event structure data source (like ESPN or the Academy Awards website) allows Phase 2 efficiency gains without any betting association.
	



Monetization provocations
Provocation
	Hypothesis
	What if the aggregate prediction data is worth more than the product itself?
	At scale, Melee’s pick data is a uniquely valuable signal for media companies, brands, pollsters, and betting markets. Requires clean data hygiene from day one.
	What if the Host is the customer, not the group?
	A Host Pro tier — paid by the one person who gets the most value — will convert at higher rates than a group subscription.
	What if customization makes Host Pro feel fun rather than transactional?
	Premium avatar packs, exclusive elimination styles, and custom reveal mechanics make the paid tier expressive. The Host is buying personality, not features.
	

WHAT WOWS — Moments of Delight
	

What Wows identifies the moments that generated the most energy in the beta — and projects what those moments could become in Melee. These are the product’s emotional peaks. Everything else is scaffolding.


Wow moments: Beta → Melee
The moment (beta)
	What it becomes in Melee
	Chatter erupts
When picks became visible and results rolled in, the comment section came alive. Most-used feature after picks themselves. Friends who had gone quiet returned.
	The staggered reveal
Picks revealed progressively. Each reveal is a micro-moment. The group watches together as picks — and reputations — are exposed.
	The leaderboard shift
An upset flipped the leaderboard and the person in last suddenly had a path to win. Engagement spiked. Stakes felt real.
	Pick change receipts
The group sees when and what a member changed before the deadline. Blind loyalty is noble. Last-minute hedging is ammunition.
	Seeing a friend’s picks
Browsing a friend’s bracket generated immediate reactions — disbelief, mockery, respect. The picks became a proxy for personality.
	The receipts live forever
Group history accumulates across events. The cross-event all-time leaderboard is the permanent record. History is the product’s long-term glue.
	

A melee is the moment when everyone is in it — the chaos of friends arguing about who had the right pick, whose bold take aged well, whose confident prediction aged terribly. The staggered reveal, pick change history, Chatter, and permanent leaderboard all exist to manufacture that moment reliably, for any event, for any group of friends.
	

Hypothesized wow moments — not yet tested
Moment
	Why it matters
	The loser posts first (beta-validated)
	One Chatter post after Army’s elimination came from the user who picked Army, not from winners. Chatter fires as a release valve for the losing side. If this pattern holds — and it should — the moment of loss is the product’s best Chatter activation trigger. Surface Chatter immediately when a pick is eliminated.
	The lapsed fan rekindled (beta-validated)
	A lapsed lacrosse fan bought ESPN+ after saving his bracket. Melee created downstream economic behavior outside itself. The product makes fans, not just serves them. Validates zero-knowledge entry. First unsolicited testimonial.
	The AI event creation reveal
	A Host describes the Oscars in plain English and sees a fully structured Melee appear. The magic of setup sets the tone for everything that follows.
	The casual friend wins
	The autofill user tops the leaderboard without watching a single game. The product’s best viral moment and most powerful argument against expertise-gating. Design for it explicitly.
	The cross-event leaderboard
	A Group’s all-time record across lacrosse, the Oscars, and Survivor. History as identity. The moment a Group sees their multi-event record, Melee becomes their place.
	The Host sets the tone
	A Host picks ‘Skeleton’ as the elimination style and ‘worst-to-best’ reveal order. The Melee feels like theirs before a single pick is made.
	

WHAT WORKS — Validated Decisions
	

What Works captures decisions that have been made and assumptions that have been validated — through the accidental beta or deliberate product reasoning. Each decision includes the rationale and the risk if the assumption is wrong.


Validated by the beta
Decision
	Validated assumption
	Risk if wrong
	Supabase as backend
	Production-ready at friend-group scale. Free tier covers MVP. Proven in beta.
	Schema migration is painful if not done right the first time.
	Chatter as core feature
	Most-used feature after picks in beta. Social layer drives more engagement than the event.
	Moderation becomes a problem at scale. Not an MVP concern.
	AI-assisted development
	Full beta built in days with no traditional coding. Viable for MVP build.
	Complex architectural decisions (schema, RLS) still require careful human judgment.
	

Decided by design reasoning — documented in Brief v1.2
Decision
	Rationale
	Risk if wrong
	Group-scoped picks at DB level from day one
	Privacy enforced at DB level via Supabase RLS. Retrofitting later is costly and risky.
	Slightly more complex schema upfront. Worth it.
	Confidence staking removed
	Conflicts with Melee’s ethos: certainty, stubbornness, blind allegiance. No one says “I’m 87% confident.”
	Removes a potential engagement signal. Revisitable in Phase 3.
	Open beta: public launch, no waitlist
	Maximum user data is more valuable than controlled rollout. Network effects require scale.
	Harder to manage quality without controlled rollout. Acceptable trade-off.
	Host Pro as Phase 4 monetization lead
	Directly monetizes the person who gets the most value. Everyone else plays free.
	Willingness to pay unvalidated until meaningful user base exists.
	Staggered pick reveal (not simultaneous)
	Progressive revelation creates drama. Each reveal is a social moment, not a data dump.
	More complex to build. Requires careful UX design.
	Host customization as Phase 2
	Phase 1 ships with opinionated defaults. Customization is an option, never a requirement.
	Defaults must be good enough to carry Phase 1 without customization.
	Platform nomenclature: domain-neutral throughout
	Stage/Contender/Outcome works across sports, Oscars, Survivor. Format-specific labels defined by Host per-Melee.
	Unfamiliar terms may confuse early users. Mitigated by clear UI labels.
	AI event creation uses user-provided source domain
	Asking ‘who hosts this event?’ is natural and gives AI a clean starting point. Host reviews draft before anything goes live.
	AI may produce incorrect structures for niche events. Review step is the guardrail.
	Polymarket integration deferred to Phase 2
	Too complex for MVP. Phase 1 AI creation must be architected with Polymarket data model in mind.
	Missing event discovery in Phase 1. Acceptable given MVP constraints.
	Aggregate data as Phase 5 revenue hypothesis
	Prediction data at scale is valuable to media, brands, and betting markets. Requires clean data hygiene from day one.
	Speculative until meaningful scale. Long-term play only.
	Groups use user-defined names
	The identity of the group is theirs. ‘The Infallible Ones’ carries more personality than any system label.
	No risk. Pure upside.
	Multi-group mental model: Host is the unit of loyalty
	Each person has multiple friend groups with different interests. Group composition is event-dependent. The Host running multiple Melees across different groups is multiple acquisition events per year, each reaching new people.
	If Hosts burn out from setup burden, multi-group model fails. AI event creation must be genuinely fast — under 5 minutes.
	Chatter must be persistent and accessible from every screen
	Host found Chatter buried at the bottom of the tab. The impulse to trash talk fires in a moment and dies if Chatter requires navigation. Design mandate, not a preference.
	Increases mobile layout complexity. Solve with floating action button or persistent bottom nav. Do not defer.
	Spectator mode: invite-only read-only access
	Formalizes observed ‘app shown to outsider’ viral behavior. Spectators see leaderboard, bracket, and Chatter. Cannot submit picks or post Chatter. Converts observers into future Hosts or participants.
	Spectator scope must be strictly read-only at MVP. Any write access creates a two-tier participation problem.
	

Open questions — to be resolved in Phase 1
Question
	Status
	Notes
	Pick reveal timing: staggered or immediate?
	✅ Resolved
	Picks visible immediately on submission. Pre-event transparency drives Chatter. The deadline is the lock, not a reveal. Last-minute pick changes are the mind game.
	Result entry UX: Host-only or group suggestion + Host confirm?
	✅ Resolved
	Host-only at MVP. Group suggestion + Host confirm is Phase 2. AI auto-results is also Phase 2.
	Host customization: per-Melee or per-Group standing preference?
	✅ Resolved
	Per-Melee. The tone of an Oscars ballot differs from an NFL bracket. Host sets the culture of each specific Melee.
	Comment edit function?
	✅ Resolved
	No edit. By design. Your words stand. Confirmation step before posting is the UX solution.
	Tie-break method?
	✅ Resolved
	Host selects at event creation: Prop Question (default), Submission Timestamp, or Closest to the Pin. All fully automatic. Watch closely in Phase 1 beta.
	Melee vs betting positioning?
	✅ Resolved
	Melee is the anti-bet. Brand pillar: communal, social stakes, game night. Polymarket referenced only as event structure data source — no betting association.
	AI event creation fallback: niche events the AI can’t scaffold?
	📋 Open
	Host fills in the blanks after AI scaffolds what it can. Fallback UX needs design in Phase 1.
	Cross-event leaderboard: when does it launch?
	📋 Open
	Requires at least two completed Melees in the same Group. Phase 3 target. Schema must support it from day one.
	Chatter vs. text: is persistent Chatter enough to activate in the moment?
	Bet placed
	Melee bets on context and permanence over feature parity. Validate in MVP beta — watch whether Chatter engagement rises when surfaced persistently everywhere.
	Spectator mode: scope and rollout timing?
	Open
	Invite-only, read-only at MVP. No picks, no Chatter posting. Rollout with Groups feature. Watch whether spectators convert to participants in next Melees.
	B2B/media partnership: when is consumer traction strong enough?
	Open
	Phase 4+ opportunity. Need a behavior story: users who played one Melee averaged X more within six months. Start with mid-tier media partner. Major network is the inbound goal, not the cold pitch.
	

Post-Tournament Survey
Send within 24 hours of the final result. Emotional memory fades fast. No more than 10 questions — these are friends, not research subjects. Every question resolves something in the beta observation table or the MVP build. Mix of quick-answer and one open-ended question per section; the open-ended ones are where the gold is.

	Format / Options
