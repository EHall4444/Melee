RAW NOTES ONLY. This is the scratchpad — unfiltered observations from the Bracket Battle beta. Synthesis and pattern recognition happen in the Design Thinking doc. The only rule: keep observations and inferences in separate columns. What you saw goes in Observation. What you think it means goes in Notes / Inference.
	

How to Use This Log

TYPE key:  OBSERVATION = what happened, directly witnessed or measured  ·  INFERENCE = interpretation of an observation (flag these)  ·  QUESTION = something this raises that needs answering  ·  SIGNAL = a moment that directly maps to a What Wows or What Works item in the design thinking doc
	

Observation Log
Early rounds  May 2026
⚠ CONFOUND: Round 1 data is unreliable for engagement conclusions.
The product was actively being developed and shipped during Round 1. Bug fixes, UI changes, and gameplay discussions were happening live in Chatter throughout the observation window. Users were reacting to a moving target, not a stable product. This almost certainly suppressed organic engagement and inflates the host post count (many host posts were admin and development announcements, not natural host behavior). The Chatter drop-off observation cannot be cleanly attributed to low early-round stakes vs. development interruption — the two effects cannot be separated from this data.
The Quarterfinals are effectively Round 1 for observation purposes. Stable product, real stakes, users who know how it works. Draw no conclusions from Round 1 engagement data. Use it for context only.

	OBSERVATION
	Chatter has dropped off in early rounds of the tournament.
	Likely early-round effect, not product failure. Low stakes = low drama = low Chatter. Watch for spike after upsets and leaderboard shifts. Do not draw conclusions until semifinals.
	May 2026
	Group behavior
	INFERENCE
	Users may be opting for text over Chatter.
	INFERENCE ONLY — not directly observed. Simpler explanation: texting is the default social reflex; the group already has a thread and switching to Chatter has friction. Do NOT interpret as demand for richer media (gifs, photos, font formatting) without direct user evidence. Survey Q2 will help diagnose.
	May 2026
	Expert
	SIGNAL
	User — lacrosse fan, infrequent contact — encountered a login issue. When informed it was being fixed, responded unprompted: “I just like the banter.”
	User has genuine event knowledge and real bracket stakes. Primary stated motivation was the social layer, not the picks or leaderboard. Login friction did not kill engagement — pull-back survived a technical barrier. Watch whether he returns after the fix and whether he chatters when he does.
	May 2026
	Host
	SIGNAL
	No timestamp visible on Chatter posts. In a multi-day event, users cannot orient themselves temporally — a comment from two days ago looks identical to one from five minutes ago.
	The timestamp problem only exists if the product is working. Users are returning to Chatter across multiple days — that is the real signal. Proposed UX resolution: timestamp on each Chatter post. Resolve in MVP build.
	May 2026
	Close Friend / Expert
	SIGNAL
	One unprompted Chatter post following Army’s elimination. Post came from the user who had picked Army to win. No post from any winning picker.
	Loser posted, not winner. Chatter is functioning as a release valve for the losing side. Winners let the leaderboard speak. Watch whether this pattern holds: if losers consistently drive Chatter, it has implications for how the UI surfaces the feed after a result drops — the moment of loss may be the product’s best activation trigger.
	May 8–12
	App data / Chatter export
	OBSERVATION
	Early-round Chatter analysis (PRELIMINARY — do not draw conclusions until semifinals): 55 total posts across 5 days. Excluding host (ehall), 26 organic posts from 9 non-host users. 6 of 9 non-host users posted at least once (67% organic participation rate). testes_73 led non-host posting with 11 posts.
	INFERENCE: Healthy baseline but host-dependent. ehall drove 53% of all posts. Without host facilitation, organic rate and volume drop significantly. The product needs to generate moments that prompt posting — not rely on a highly engaged host. Do not conclude until quarterfinals and beyond.
	May 8–12
	Chatter export
	OBSERVATION
	Post type breakdown (non-host, early rounds): trash talk / banter 46%, self-deprecation / casual voice 19%, product feedback disguised as banter 15%, unsolicited request for money stakes 8%, other 12%.
	Product feedback hidden inside jokes: picks visibility table, edit function, payment app. All genuine requests. testes_73’s request for a table showing who picked what (“would materially streamline my trash talking game”) is a direct feature request for pick transparency UI. Flag for MVP design.
	May 8–12
	Chatter export + persona context
	SIGNAL
	petegwill (neighbor, zero lacrosse knowledge, zero Chatter posts) led the leaderboard after round one. Nobody announced this. The group never reacted to it in Chatter.
	This is the product’s core thesis — casual friend winning — playing out silently. The moment happened and nobody knew. The product needs to surface and amplify this: a leaderboard notification, a Chatter prompt, something. Watch whether petegwill holds position through the quarters. If he does and the group finds out, record the reaction in full.
	May 8–12
	Chatter export + persona context
	INFERENCE
	Three knowledgeable silent users (flagdown, edesjardins, magnum) posted little or nothing despite having strong domain knowledge and opinions.
	INFERENCE: Silent experts need a trigger. They have opinions — they just didn’t have a moment that surfaced those opinions publicly. Pick transparency table (who picked what for the next matchup) would activate exactly this group. Watch whether upsets and leaderboard shifts pull them in during later rounds.
	May 8–12
	alastairlovespapa (6yo son)
	SIGNAL
	A 6-year-old user made all picks by mascot after being told team names. His father posted an apology to Chatter. Mascot images were not available in the UI.
	Mascot images are a legitimate product requirement, not cosmetic. Picking by mascot is a real zero-knowledge strategy and the resulting Chatter (“I apologize for my son’s picks”) is some of the best content in the dataset. Adds personality and gives non-expert users a genuine decision framework. Flag for Jeff’s team.
	May 9
	speabsgmailcom + testes_73
	SIGNAL
	speabsgmailcom asked unprompted for a $10 buy-in. testes_73 supported it and tagged jeffycakes to build a payment app. Neither was prompted. Both are former lacrosse players and competitive personalities.
	Organic demand for stakes from two users independently. Nobody suggested this. The social pressure mechanic is working well enough that users want to raise the temperature. Melee’s anti-bet brand position holds — this is bragging rights competition, not gambling. But the desire for stakes is worth noting for Host Pro monetization thinking.
